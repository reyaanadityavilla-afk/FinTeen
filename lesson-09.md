# LESSON 9: COMPOUND INTEREST

## Introduction

Imagine you have two choices.

### Choice 1

You receive:

₹10,000

today.

No growth.

No interest.

After 20 years:

You still have:

₹10,000

---

### Choice 2

You invest:

₹10,000

and earn interest every year.

Not only does your original money grow, but the interest itself begins earning interest.

Over time, growth accelerates dramatically.

This process is called:

**Compound Interest.**

Albert Einstein is often credited with calling compound interest:

> "The eighth wonder of the world."

Whether or not he actually said it, the idea captures an important truth:

Compound interest is one of the most powerful forces in finance.

Many wealthy investors, business owners, and savers have benefited enormously from compounding.

Understanding compound interest is essential because it explains how small amounts of money can become large amounts over time.

---

# What Is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest earned on both:

* The original amount invested
* Previously earned interest

In simple words:

Your money earns money, and then that money earns money too.

This creates a snowball effect.

Over time, growth becomes faster and faster.

---

# Simple Interest vs Compound Interest

Before understanding compound interest, let's compare it with simple interest.

---

# Simple Interest

Simple interest is earned only on the original amount.

Example:

Investment:

₹10,000

Interest Rate:

10%

Annual Interest:

₹1,000

After Year 1:

₹11,000

After Year 2:

₹12,000

After Year 3:

₹13,000

Interest remains constant.

---

# Compound Interest

Compound interest earns interest on the growing balance.

Example:

Investment:

₹10,000

Interest Rate:

10%

---

Year 1:

₹11,000

---

Year 2:

₹12,100

---

Year 3:

₹13,310

---

Year 4:

₹14,641

Growth accelerates because interest earns additional interest.

---

# Why Compound Interest Is Powerful

At first, compound growth appears slow.

This causes many people to underestimate it.

However, as time passes, growth begins accelerating.

Think of rolling a snowball down a hill.

Initially:

Small growth.

Later:

Rapid growth.

Compounding works similarly.

# The Three Ingredients of Compound Interest

Compound interest depends mainly on three factors.

---

# Factor 1: Principal

Principal is the original amount invested.

Example:

₹10,000

The larger the principal, the greater the potential growth.

---

# Factor 2: Interest Rate

The interest rate determines how quickly money grows.

Example:

5%

10%

15%

Higher rates generally produce faster growth.

---

# Factor 3: Time

Time is the most powerful factor.

Many people focus on:

Money.

Successful investors focus on:

Time.

The longer money compounds, the greater the effect.

---

---

---

## Investor B

More time for compounding.

Time is often more valuable than investment size.

Example:

₹1,000 interest.

Now:

The original investment earns interest.

The ₹1,000 earns interest.

Growth accelerates.

This process repeats continuously.

---

# The Rule of 72

The Rule of 72 is a quick way to estimate how long money takes to double.

Formula:

72 ÷ Interest Rate

---

# Example

Interest Rate:

8%

72 ÷ 8 = 9

Money approximately doubles every:

9 years

---

# Example

Interest Rate:

12%

72 ÷ 12 = 6

Money approximately doubles every:

6 years

This rule is widely used in finance.
