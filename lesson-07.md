# LESSON 7: LEDGERS

## Introduction

Imagine you run a small business.

During one week, the following transactions occur:

* You invest ₹50,000 into the business.
* You purchase equipment worth ₹10,000.
* You sell products worth ₹15,000.
* You pay rent of ₹2,000.
* You buy inventory worth ₹5,000.

After a month, someone asks:

* How much cash remains?
* How much inventory do you have?
* How much rent did you pay?
* How much revenue did you earn?

You cannot simply rely on memory.

Businesses need a systematic way to record and organize financial transactions.

This is where ledgers become important.

A ledger is one of the most fundamental tools in accounting.

Before financial statements such as:

* Income Statements
* Balance Sheets
* Cash Flow Statements

can be prepared, transactions must first be recorded and organized.

That organization happens in the ledger.

Without ledgers:

* Financial reporting would be impossible.
* Accounting would be chaotic.
* Businesses would struggle to track money.

Understanding ledgers is essential because they form the backbone of accounting systems.

---

# What Is a Ledger?

A ledger is a book or record that contains all transactions related to a specific account.

In simple words:

A ledger organizes financial transactions into categories.

Instead of recording everything in one place, accountants separate transactions into accounts.

Examples:

* Cash Account
* Rent Account
* Sales Account
* Inventory Account
* Salary Account

Each account has its own ledger.

---

# Why Ledgers Matter

Imagine a business completes:

10,000 transactions

in a year.

Without ledgers:

Finding information would be extremely difficult.

Ledgers help businesses:

* Organize transactions
* Track balances
* Prepare financial statements
* Detect errors

---

# The Accounting Process

The accounting process generally follows this order:

### Transaction Occurs

↓

### Journal Entry Created

↓

### Posted to Ledger

↓

### Trial Balance Prepared

↓

### Financial Statements Created

The ledger sits at the center of the accounting system.

---

# Journal vs Ledger

Students often confuse journals and ledgers.

---

## Journal

Transactions are recorded in chronological order.

Example:

June 1: Bought inventory.

June 2: Paid rent.

June 3: Made sale.

---

## Ledger

Transactions are organized by account.

Example:

Cash Account

Sales Account

Rent Account

Inventory Account

The ledger provides organization.

---

# What Is a Ledger Account?

A ledger account records all transactions related to a specific account.

Examples:

* Cash Ledger
* Inventory Ledger
* Equipment Ledger
* Revenue Ledger

Each ledger tracks increases and decreases.

---

# The T-Account

Ledgers are often illustrated using T-accounts.

Example:

```
Cash Account

Debit | Credit
-------|-------
₹50,000 |
        | ₹10,000
```

The T shape helps accountants visualize transactions.

---

# Debit and Credit Review

Every ledger entry follows the double-entry accounting system.

Each transaction affects at least two accounts.

Every transaction includes:

### Debit

and

### Credit

Total Debits must always equal Total Credits.

---

# Example Transaction

Owner invests:

₹1,00,000

in the business.

---

Journal Entry:

Debit Cash ₹1,00,000

Credit Owner's Capital ₹1,00,000

---

Ledger Effect:

Cash Ledger increases.

Capital Ledger increases.

---

# Cash Ledger

One of the most important ledger accounts.

Tracks:

* Cash received
* Cash spent

---

### Example

Cash Ledger

| Date   | Description        | Debit   | Credit  | Balance |
| ------ | ------------------ | ------- | ------- | ------- |
| Jan 1  | Owner Investment   | ₹50,000 |         | ₹50,000 |
| Jan 5  | Equipment Purchase |         | ₹10,000 | ₹40,000 |
| Jan 10 | Sales Revenue      | ₹15,000 |         | ₹55,000 |

The ledger continuously updates the balance.

---

# Sales Ledger

Tracks revenue transactions.

Example:

Sales Ledger

| Date   | Description   | Debit | Credit  |
| ------ | ------------- | ----- | ------- |
| Jan 10 | Product Sales |       | ₹15,000 |
| Jan 12 | Product Sales |       | ₹5,000  |

Total Sales:

₹20,000

---

# Rent Ledger

Tracks rent expenses.

Example:

Rent Ledger

| Date  | Description | Debit  | Credit |
| ----- | ----------- | ------ | ------ |
| Jan 5 | Office Rent | ₹2,000 |        |

Rent expenses accumulate here.

---

# Inventory Ledger

Tracks inventory purchases and usage.

Example:

Inventory Ledger

| Date   | Description        | Debit   | Credit |
| ------ | ------------------ | ------- | ------ |
| Jan 2  | Inventory Purchase | ₹10,000 |        |
| Jan 15 | Inventory Used     |         | ₹4,000 |

Inventory Balance:

₹6,000

---

# Accounts Receivable Ledger

Tracks money owed by customers.

Example:

Customer purchases:

₹20,000

on credit.

Accounts Receivable increases.

When payment is received:

Accounts Receivable decreases.

---

# Accounts Payable Ledger

Tracks money owed to suppliers.

Example:

Business purchases inventory.

Payment occurs later.

Accounts Payable records the obligation.

---

# General Ledger

The General Ledger is the master accounting record.

It contains all individual ledger accounts.

Examples:

* Cash
* Revenue
* Expenses
* Assets
* Liabilities
* Equity

Every transaction eventually appears in the General Ledger.

---

# Subsidiary Ledgers

Large businesses often use subsidiary ledgers.

These provide detailed breakdowns.

Examples:

### Customer Ledger

Tracks each customer separately.

### Supplier Ledger

Tracks each supplier separately.

### Inventory Ledger

Tracks individual inventory items.

---

# Posting to the Ledger

Posting means transferring information from the journal to the ledger.

Example:

Journal Entry:

Debit Cash ₹5,000

Credit Sales ₹5,000

The accountant posts:

₹5,000 to Cash Ledger

₹5,000 to Sales Ledger

This keeps accounts organized.

---

# Ledger Balances

Each ledger eventually has a balance.

Examples:

Cash Balance:

₹1,00,000

Inventory Balance:

₹20,000

Revenue Balance:

₹50,000

These balances help prepare financial statements.

---

# Trial Balance

After ledger balances are calculated:

A Trial Balance is created.

Purpose:

Verify that:

Total Debits = Total Credits

If the totals do not match:

An accounting error likely exists.

---

# Example Trial Balance

| Account   | Debit   | Credit  |
| --------- | ------- | ------- |
| Cash      | ₹50,000 |         |
| Inventory | ₹10,000 |         |
| Equipment | ₹20,000 |         |
| Capital   |         | ₹80,000 |

Totals:

₹80,000 = ₹80,000

Balanced.
