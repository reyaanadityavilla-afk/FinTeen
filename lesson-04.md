# LESSON 4: CORPORATE FINANCE

## Introduction

Imagine you become the CEO of a company.

Every day, you face important questions:

* Should we build a new factory?
* Should we launch a new product?
* Should we borrow money?
* Should we issue shares?
* Should we acquire another company?
* Should we invest ₹100 Crore into expansion?

These decisions can determine whether a company succeeds or fails.

The area of finance responsible for making these decisions is called:

**Corporate Finance.**

Corporate Finance is the branch of finance that focuses on managing a company's money and making financial decisions that increase company value.

Unlike Investment Banking, which advises companies, Corporate Finance works inside the company itself.

Corporate finance professionals help answer one central question:

> How can a company maximize its value?

Understanding Corporate Finance is essential because it forms the foundation of:

* Business Management
* Investment Banking
* Private Equity
* Entrepreneurship
* Financial Leadership

---

# What Is Corporate Finance?

Corporate Finance is the area of finance concerned with managing a company's financial resources and making decisions that maximize shareholder value.

In simple words:

Corporate Finance helps businesses decide:

* How to make money
* How to spend money
* How to invest money
* How to raise money

---

# The Main Goal of Corporate Finance

The primary objective is:

### Maximize Shareholder Value

Shareholders own the company.

Corporate finance aims to increase the value of that ownership over time.

---

# What Does a Corporate Finance Team Do?

Corporate Finance teams help companies make major financial decisions.

Their responsibilities often include:

* Budgeting
* Forecasting
* Raising capital
* Managing cash
* Evaluating investments
* Financial planning

---

# The Three Major Decisions in Corporate Finance

Corporate Finance revolves around three core decisions:

### Investment Decisions

### Financing Decisions

### Dividend Decisions

These form the foundation of the field.

---

# Investment Decisions

Investment decisions involve deciding where to invest company money.

Questions include:

* Should we open a new branch?
* Should we build a factory?
* Should we launch a product?
* Should we buy another company?

The goal is to invest in projects that generate more value than they cost.

---

# Example

Investment Required:

₹10 Crore

Expected Future Profit:

₹25 Crore

This may be a good investment.

Corporate Finance professionals analyze opportunities like this.

---

# Financing Decisions

Once a company decides to invest, it needs money.

The next question becomes:

How should the company obtain that money?

---

# Option 1: Debt Financing

Borrow money.

Examples:

* Bank loans
* Bonds

Advantages:

* Owners keep ownership

Disadvantages:

* Interest payments

---

# Option 2: Equity Financing

Sell ownership.

Examples:

* Shares
* Investors

Advantages:

* No repayment

Disadvantages:

* Ownership dilution

Corporate Finance determines the best mix.

---

# Dividend Decisions

After earning profits, companies must decide:

Should profits be distributed?

Or reinvested?

This is called the dividend decision.

---

# Example

Profit:

₹100 Crore

Option A:

Pay dividends.

---

Option B:

Reinvest into expansion.

Corporate Finance helps determine the optimal choice.

---

# What Is Capital?

Capital refers to money used for business activities.

Examples:

* Investor money
* Loans
* Retained earnings

Businesses need capital to grow.

---

# Capital Allocation

One of the most important concepts in Corporate Finance.

Capital Allocation means deciding where company money should go.

Examples:

* Marketing
* Expansion
* Technology
* Acquisitions

Great companies allocate capital efficiently.

---

# Capital Structure

Capital Structure refers to how a company finances itself.

Formula:

### Debt + Equity = Capital Structure

---

# Example

Debt:

₹40 Crore

Equity:

₹60 Crore

Total Capital:

₹100 Crore

Corporate Finance determines the ideal balance.

---

# Why Capital Structure Matters

Too much debt creates risk.

Too little debt may limit growth.

Finding the right balance is critical.

---

# Cost of Capital

Money is not free.

Every source of capital has a cost.

Examples:

Bank Loan:

Interest Cost

Investor Money:

Ownership Cost

This cost is called:

### Cost of Capital

---

# Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)

One of the most important concepts in Corporate Finance.

WACC represents the average cost of all company funding sources.

Formula:

Simplified:

### WACC = Average Cost of Debt and Equity

---

# Why WACC Matters

Companies must earn returns greater than WACC.

Otherwise:

Value is destroyed.

---

# Example

WACC:

10%

Investment Return:

15%

Good investment.

---

Investment Return:

7%

Poor investment.

---

# Capital Budgeting

Capital Budgeting is the process of evaluating large investment projects.

Examples:

* New factories
* Technology systems
* Product launches

The goal is to determine whether projects create value.

---

# Net Present Value (NPV)

One of the most important Corporate Finance tools.

NPV measures whether a project creates value.

---

# Basic Idea

Future money is worth less than money today.

Corporate Finance adjusts future cash flows to today's value.

---

# Example

Investment:

₹1 Crore

Future Cash Flow:

₹1.5 Crore

NPV helps determine whether this investment is worthwhile.

---

# Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

Another important metric.

IRR estimates the return generated by an investment.

Higher IRR generally means a more attractive project.

---

# Example

Project A:

IRR = 12%

Project B:

IRR = 20%

Project B may be more attractive.

---

# Corporate Finance and Cash Management

Companies must manage cash carefully.

Cash is needed for:

* Salaries
* Rent
* Inventory
* Loans

Poor cash management can create serious problems.

---

# Working Capital Management

Working Capital:

### Current Assets − Current Liabilities

Corporate Finance teams monitor:

* Inventory
* Accounts Receivable
* Accounts Payable

Efficient management improves cash flow.

---

# Financial Planning

Companies constantly forecast the future.

Questions include:

* How much revenue will we earn?
* What will expenses be?
* How much cash will we need?

Financial planning helps companies prepare.

---

# Financial Forecasting

Forecasting estimates future performance.

Common forecasts include:

* Revenue
* Expenses
* Profit
* Cash Flow

Executives use forecasts to make decisions.

---

# Risk Management

Every business faces risks.

Examples:

* Economic downturns
* Competition
* Currency fluctuations
* Interest rate changes

Corporate Finance helps identify and manage these risks.

---

# Mergers and Acquisitions

Corporate Finance often evaluates acquisitions.

Questions include:

* Should we buy this company?
* Is the price reasonable?
* Will it increase value?

These decisions can transform businesses.

---

# Corporate Finance vs Investment Banking

Students often confuse them.

---

# Corporate Finance

Works inside the company.

Makes decisions for the company.

---

# Investment Banking

Advises companies.

Provides external expertise.

---

# Example

Apple CFO:

Corporate Finance.

---

Goldman Sachs Advisor:

Investment Banking.

# Key Takeaways

* Corporate Finance focuses on managing company finances and maximizing shareholder value.
* The three major decisions are Investment, Financing, and Dividend Decisions.
* Capital Structure determines how companies finance operations.
* WACC measures the cost of capital.
* Capital Budgeting evaluates large investments.
* NPV and IRR are key project evaluation tools.
* Working Capital Management improves liquidity.
* Corporate Finance teams manage cash, risk, and investments.
* CFOs lead corporate financial strategy.
* Corporate Finance influences nearly every major business decision.
* Understanding Corporate Finance is essential for advanced finance and business leadership.
