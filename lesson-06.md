# LESSON 6: VALUATION

## Introduction

Imagine someone comes to you and says:

"I want to buy your company."

Your first question will probably be:

**"How much is it worth?"**

Now imagine you are:

* An investor evaluating a startup
* An investment banker advising a merger
* A venture capitalist investing in a company
* A private equity firm buying a business
* A shareholder buying stock

All of them ask the same question:

**What is this company worth?**

The process of answering this question is called:

# Valuation

Valuation is one of the most important concepts in all of finance.

Investment bankers, venture capitalists, private equity investors, hedge funds, corporate executives, and stock market investors all rely on valuation.

Every acquisition.

Every investment.

Every IPO.

Every fundraising round.

Every stock purchase.

Depends on valuation.

Understanding valuation is one of the biggest steps from beginner finance to advanced finance.

---

# What Is Valuation?

Valuation is the process of determining the economic value of a company, asset, investment, or business.

In simple words:

Valuation answers:

### "What is something worth?"

---

# Why Valuation Matters

Imagine buying a company.

If the company is worth:

₹100 Crore

but you pay:

₹200 Crore

You likely made a poor investment.

---

If the company is worth:

₹100 Crore

and you buy it for:

₹70 Crore

You may have found a good investment.

Valuation helps investors avoid overpaying.

---

# What Can Be Valued?

Valuation applies to many things.

Examples:

* Companies
* Stocks
* Startups
* Real Estate
* Bonds
* Businesses
* Intellectual Property

Anything with economic value can be valued.

---

# Why Different People Care About Valuation

---

# Investors

Want to know:

Is this investment worth buying?

---

# Founders

Want to know:

How much is my company worth?

---

# Venture Capitalists

Want to know:

What ownership should I receive?

---

# Investment Bankers

Want to know:

What is a fair acquisition price?

---

# Private Equity Firms

Want to know:

Can this company generate returns?

---

# The Core Principle of Valuation

At its heart:

A company is worth the money it can generate in the future.

Future profits.

Future cash flows.

Future growth.

These ultimately determine value.

---

# Enterprise Value vs Equity Value

One of the most important concepts.

---

# Equity Value

Value belonging to shareholders.

Formula:

### Equity Value = Share Price × Shares Outstanding

---

# Example

Shares Outstanding:

10 Crore

Share Price:

₹100

Equity Value:

₹1,000 Crore

---

# Enterprise Value

Represents the value of the entire business.

Formula:

### Enterprise Value = Equity Value + Debt − Cash

---

# Example

Equity Value:

₹1,000 Crore

Debt:

₹300 Crore

Cash:

₹100 Crore

Enterprise Value:

₹1,200 Crore

Investment bankers use Enterprise Value constantly.

---

# The Three Main Valuation Methods

These are the methods used on Wall Street.

---

# 1. Comparable Company Analysis (Comps)

Most common method.

---

# What Is It?

Compare a company to similar companies.

Example:

To value a finance company:

Compare it to other finance companies.

---

# Example

Company A:

Worth ₹500 Crore

Revenue:

₹100 Crore

Valuation Multiple:

5× Revenue

---

Your company:

Revenue:

₹50 Crore

Estimated Value:

₹250 Crore

This is called comparable analysis.

---

# Why It Works

Similar companies often trade at similar valuations.

Investors compare:

* Growth
* Profitability
* Industry

---

# Common Multiples

---

# Price-to-Earnings (P/E)

Formula:

### Share Price ÷ Earnings Per Share

One of the most famous valuation ratios.

---

# Example

Share Price:

₹100

EPS:

₹10

P/E:

10×

---

# Enterprise Value to Revenue

Formula:

### Enterprise Value ÷ Revenue

Common for startups.

---

# Enterprise Value to EBITDA

Formula:

### Enterprise Value ÷ EBITDA

Widely used in investment banking.

---

# What Is EBITDA?

EBITDA stands for:

Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization.

It measures business operating performance.

Investment bankers use EBITDA constantly.

---

# 2. Precedent Transactions

Another common method.

---

# What Is It?

Look at previous acquisitions.

Example:

If similar companies were sold for:

10× EBITDA

That provides a benchmark.

---

# Why It Matters

Real buyers paid real prices.

This often provides valuable insight.

---

# Example

Company X sold for:

₹500 Crore

EBITDA:

₹50 Crore

Acquisition Multiple:

10×

Use this information to estimate value.

---

# 3. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

The most advanced valuation method.

Many investment bankers consider it the most important.

---

# Core Idea

A company's value equals the present value of future cash flows.

---

# Why Present Value Matters

Money today is worth more than money tomorrow.

Example:

Would you rather receive:

₹100 today

or

₹100 in five years?

Most people choose today.

This concept drives DCF valuation.

---

# Time Value of Money

One of finance's most important principles.

Money can be invested.

Money can earn returns.

Therefore:

Future money is worth less than current money.

---

# DCF Process

Step 1:

Forecast future cash flows.

---

Step 2:

Estimate growth.

---

Step 3:

Apply a discount rate.

---

Step 4:

Calculate present value.

---

Step 5:

Add values together.

---

Result:

Estimated company value.

---

# Discount Rate

The discount rate reflects:

* Risk
* Opportunity cost
* Expected returns

Higher risk:

Higher discount rate.

---

# Example

Safe government bond:

Low discount rate.

---

Startup:

High discount rate.

Because startup risk is much greater.

---

# Startup Valuation

Startups are difficult to value.

Why?

Because:

* Limited profits
* Limited history
* High uncertainty

VCs often focus on:

* Market size
* Growth
* Team
* Potential

rather than traditional financial metrics.

---

# Example

FinTeen:

Revenue:

₹5 Lakh

Current Profit:

₹0

Traditional valuation might appear low.

However:

Potential Users:

500 Million Students

This future potential could create a much higher valuation.

---

# Pre-Money Valuation

Used heavily in Venture Capital.

Definition:

Company value before investment.

---

# Example

Startup Value:

₹10 Crore

Investment:

₹2 Crore

Pre-Money Valuation:

₹10 Crore

---

# Post-Money Valuation

Value after investment.

Formula:

### Pre-Money + Investment

---

# Example

Pre-Money:

₹10 Crore

Investment:

₹2 Crore

Post-Money:

₹12 Crore

---

# Ownership Calculation

Formula:

### Investment ÷ Post-Money Valuation

---

# Example

Investment:

₹2 Crore

Post-Money:

₹12 Crore

Ownership:

16.7%

---

# Why Growth Matters

Investors often pay high valuations for growth.

Example:

Two companies.

---

Company A:

Profit:

₹50 Crore

Growth:

2%

---

Company B:

Profit:

₹20 Crore

Growth:

50%

Company B may receive a higher valuation.

Investors pay for future potential.

---

# Valuation and Stock Prices

Stock prices reflect market valuation.

When investors expect:

* Higher profits
* Faster growth

Valuations often increase.

---

# Valuation and IPOs

Before an IPO:

Investment bankers determine valuation.

Questions include:

* What price should shares be sold for?
* How much is the company worth?

Valuation drives the IPO process.

---

# Valuation and Private Equity

PE firms rely heavily on valuation.

They ask:

Can we buy this company below its future value?

If yes:

The investment may be attractive.

---

# Valuation and Mergers

Before acquiring a company:

Buyers perform extensive valuation analysis.

Overpaying can destroy shareholder value.

---

# Common Valuation Mistakes

---

# Mistake 1

Assuming revenue equals value.

Revenue alone does not determine worth.

---

# Mistake 2

Ignoring debt.

Debt affects company value significantly.

---

# Mistake 3

Ignoring growth.

Growth often drives valuation.

---

# Mistake 4

Using only one valuation method.

Professionals usually use multiple methods.

---

# Example: Valuing FinTeen

Suppose FinTeen has:

Revenue:

₹2 Crore

Growth:

100% annually

Users:

200,000

Industry Multiple:

8× Revenue

Estimated Valuation:

₹16 Crore

This is a simplified valuation approach.

Real-world valuation would involve deeper analysis.

* IPOs

# Key Takeaways

* Valuation is the process of determining what a company or asset is worth.
* Investors use valuation to avoid overpaying.
* Enterprise Value and Equity Value are fundamental concepts.
* The three major valuation methods are Comparable Companies, Precedent Transactions, and DCF.
* DCF is based on future cash flows and the time value of money.
* Startups are often valued differently from mature companies.
* Growth significantly impacts valuation.
* Valuation drives IPOs, acquisitions, venture capital, and private equity.
* Multiple valuation methods are usually used together.
* Valuation is one of the most important skills in finance, investment banking, and investing.
* Understanding valuation helps explain how the financial world prices businesses.
